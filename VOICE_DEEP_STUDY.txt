PART I — THE BIOLOGICAL MACHINE: HOW VOICE IS PHYSICALLY PRODUCED
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Voice production is a multi-system biological process. Unlike many body functions,
it repurposes structures primarily evolved for breathing and swallowing.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1.1 THE THREE SUBSYSTEMS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Voice is the product of THREE interacting subsystems:

  [A] THE RESPIRATORY SYSTEM  →  Power source (air pressure)
  [B] THE PHONATORY SYSTEM    →  Sound generator (vocal folds)
  [C] THE ARTICULATORY SYSTEM →  Sound shaper (mouth, tongue, lips, etc.)

These three systems operate simultaneously and influence each other constantly.


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1.2 SUBSYSTEM A — RESPIRATION (The Power Source)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The voice requires a constant, controlled stream of air. This air is the "fuel."

KEY STRUCTURES:
  - Diaphragm        : The main breathing muscle, dome-shaped, sits below lungs
  - Lungs            : Air reservoir
  - Intercostal muscles : Between ribs, assist in expanding/contracting chest
  - Abdominal muscles : Support exhalation pressure (core = voice power)

THE MECHANISM:
  1. Inhalation: Diaphragm contracts and flattens → chest volume expands →
     air pressure inside drops → air rushes in (passive)
  2. Exhalation for speech: Controlled, slow release of air upward through
     the trachea toward the larynx

KEY CONCEPT — Subglottal Pressure (Psg):
  The air pressure BELOW the vocal folds. This is what "pushes" the folds apart.
  Higher Psg = louder, more forceful voice.
  Lower Psg  = quieter, weaker voice.

BREATHING TYPES:
  - Clavicular    : Shallow, shoulder-raised breathing (poor for voice)
  - Thoracic      : Mid-chest breathing (average speaker)
  - Diaphragmatic : Deep belly breathing (trained singers, actors, presenters)

PRACTICAL NOTE:
  Trained speakers breathe diaphragmatically, storing larger air reserves
  and maintaining steady subglottal pressure. This results in longer phrases,
  more stable pitch, and greater projection without strain.


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1.3 SUBSYSTEM B — PHONATION (The Sound Generator)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This is where actual SOUND is born.

THE LARYNX:
  Commonly called the "voice box." Located in the front of the throat.
  Visible externally as the "Adam's apple" (more pronounced in males).
  The larynx contains the vocal folds.

  Key cartilages of the larynx:
  - Thyroid cartilage   : The large front shield; forms the Adam's apple
  - Cricoid cartilage   : Ring-shaped base, sits below thyroid
  - Arytenoid cartilages: Paired, pyramid-shaped; attach/move the vocal folds
  - Epiglottis          : Leaf-shaped flap; closes over larynx during swallowing

THE VOCAL FOLDS (Vocal Cords):
  Two paired mucous membrane folds stretched horizontally across the larynx.
  They are NOT cords — they are folds of layered tissue.

  Structure of the vocal fold (layers, from outside in):
  - Epithelium (outer mucosa) : Protective surface layer
  - Lamina propria (3 layers) : Flexible body of the fold
  - Vocalis muscle (thyroarytenoid) : The active muscular core

  The GLOTTIS is the opening between the two folds.

HOW PHONATION WORKS — The Myoelastic-Aerodynamic Theory:
  1. ADDUCTION: The arytenoid muscles bring the vocal folds together,
     closing the glottis.
  2. BUILD-UP: Subglottal air pressure builds below the closed folds.
  3. BLOW-APART: When pressure exceeds the muscular tension of the folds,
     they are blown apart (like lips buzzing on a trumpet).
  4. BERNOULLI EFFECT: As air rushes through the narrow gap, air pressure
     drops (Bernoulli principle), sucking the folds back together.
  5. OSCILLATION: This cycle repeats very rapidly, creating vibration.
  6. SOUND WAVE: This vibration creates a complex periodic sound wave —
     the raw voice (called the "buzzy" or carrier signal).

  VIBRATION RATES:
  - Adult male   : ~85–180 Hz  (cycles per second)
  - Adult female : ~165–255 Hz
  - Children     : ~250–400 Hz
  - Trained bass singers can go below 60 Hz
  - Coloratura sopranos can exceed 1,400 Hz

PITCH CONTROL:
  Pitch = the perceived frequency of vocal fold vibration.
  
  Higher pitch is achieved by:
  - Stretching the folds (cricothyroid muscle elongates them)
  - Thinning the folds (less mass per unit length → faster vibration)
  - Increasing tension

  Lower pitch is achieved by:
  - Shortening/thickening the folds (thyroarytenoid shortens them)
  - Relaxing tension

REGISTERS (vibratory modes of the folds):
  - Modal/Chest voice : Folds vibrate along their full thickness; rich, strong
  - Falsetto/Head voice: Only fold edges vibrate; higher, thinner, breathier
  - Vocal fry (Pulse) : Very slow, irregular vibration; low crackle
  - Whistle register  : Extreme high-pitched mode (rare; seen in sopranos)
  - Strohbass         : Very low reinforced register (rare; basses only)


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1.4 SUBSYSTEM C — ARTICULATION (The Sound Shaper)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The raw laryngeal sound is crude and unintelligible. Articulation transforms
it into recognizable speech sounds.

THE VOCAL TRACT:
  The resonating and shaping tube above the larynx.
  Length: ~17 cm in adult males, ~14 cm in adult females.
  
  Components:
  - Pharynx (throat cavity)
  - Oral cavity (mouth)
  - Nasal cavity
  - Lips
  - Tongue
  - Teeth
  - Hard palate (roof of mouth, front)
  - Soft palate / Velum (roof of mouth, back; movable)

ARTICULATORS (movable parts that shape sound):

  TONGUE:
    Most versatile articulator. Can shape itself into dozens of configurations.
    Zones:
    - Tip/Apex      : Forms sounds like /t/, /d/, /n/, /l/, /r/
    - Blade/Lamina  : Used for fricatives like /s/, /z/, /ʃ/ (sh)
    - Dorsum (back) : Shapes /k/, /g/, vowels, /r/ variants
    - Root          : Affects pharyngeal resonance

  LIPS:
    - Bilabial sounds: /p/, /b/, /m/ (both lips together)
    - Labiodental:     /f/, /v/ (lower lip + upper teeth)
    - Rounding:        Lip shape dramatically affects vowel quality

  VELUM (Soft Palate):
    - Raised = oral sound (air goes through mouth)
    - Lowered = nasal sound /m/, /n/, /ŋ/ (air goes through nose)
    - Partial lowering = nasalized vowels (common in French, Portuguese)

  JAW:
    Controls mouth opening. Affects vowel openness and projection.


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1.5 RESONANCE — THE AMPLIFICATION SYSTEM
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The raw glottal signal is amplified and shaped by resonating cavities.

PRIMARY RESONATORS:
  - Pharynx      : Primary shaping of vowels; depth affects warmth/brightness
  - Oral cavity  : Main resonator; size changes with tongue, jaw movement
  - Nasal cavity : Adds nasality; bone conduction creates unique color

SECONDARY RESONATORS (more debated scientifically):
  - Chest cavity : Felt as vibration in chest; contributes to low frequencies
  - Sinuses      : Bone vibration; contributes to the singer's or speaker's "ring"
  - Skull        : Overall bone conduction contributes to perceived voice quality

THE SOURCE-FILTER MODEL (Gunnar Fant, 1960):
  This is the dominant scientific model of voice production.
  
  Source  = glottal sound (buzzy tone from vocal folds)
  Filter  = vocal tract shape (determines which frequencies are amplified)
  Output  = the voice we hear

  The vocal tract has natural resonance frequencies called FORMANTS.
  Formants determine which frequencies in the glottal source are boosted.

FORMANTS:
  F1 (First Formant)  : Determined mainly by tongue height & jaw opening
                        Low F1 → high tongue position (e.g., /i/ "ee")
                        High F1 → low tongue position (e.g., /a/ "ah")
  
  F2 (Second Formant) : Determined mainly by tongue front-back position
                        High F2 → front vowels (e.g., /i/, /e/)
                        Low F2  → back vowels (e.g., /u/, /o/)
  
  F3, F4, F5          : Contribute to voice timbre, individuality, nasality
  
  The Singer's Formant : A cluster of acoustic energy around 2,500–3,500 Hz
                         produced by trained singers; allows voice to project
                         over orchestras without microphones.

PART II — ACOUSTIC ANATOMY: WHAT SOUND ACTUALLY IS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Understanding what voice IS acoustically is necessary for reproduction.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.1 FUNDAMENTAL ACOUSTIC PROPERTIES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FREQUENCY (Hz):
  Number of vibration cycles per second.
  Perceptual correlate = PITCH (how high or low a sound feels).
  
  Pitch is logarithmic: doubling frequency = one octave up.
  Musical A4 = 440 Hz. A5 = 880 Hz.

AMPLITUDE (dB):
  Magnitude of the sound pressure wave.
  Perceptual correlate = LOUDNESS / VOLUME.
  
  Human conversational speech: ~50–65 dB SPL
  Shouting:                    ~80–90 dB SPL
  Soft whisper:                ~30–40 dB SPL
  Pain threshold:               ~130 dB SPL

TIMBRE / SPECTRAL QUALITY:
  The "color" or "texture" of a voice; what makes two voices at the same
  pitch and volume still sound different.
  
  Determined by:
  - Harmonic content (relative strength of overtones)
  - Formant structure
  - Noise components (breathiness, roughness)
  - Onset/offset characteristics

DURATION:
  How long sounds, syllables, or pauses last.
  Perceptual correlate = rhythm and tempo.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.2 THE HARMONIC SERIES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

When vocal folds vibrate at a fundamental frequency (F0), they also
simultaneously produce overtones called HARMONICS.

If F0 = 100 Hz:
  H1 = 100 Hz  (fundamental)
  H2 = 200 Hz  (second harmonic)
  H3 = 300 Hz  (third harmonic)
  H4 = 400 Hz
  ... and so on, potentially up to 4,000+ Hz

The SPECTRAL ENVELOPE (the shape drawn across the tops of these harmonics)
is determined by the vocal tract filter (formants). This envelope IS the timbre.

HARMONIC RICHNESS:
  Voices with many strong high harmonics sound "bright," "edgy," "forward."
  Voices with mostly low harmonics sound "dark," "warm," "mellow."

H1-H2 RATIO:
  The difference in amplitude between the first and second harmonic is a
  key indicator of vocal register and breathiness:
  - High H1-H2 (H1 much louder than H2) = breathy, soft voice
  - Low H1-H2 (H1 and H2 similar) = pressed, strong voice

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.3 NOISE COMPONENTS IN VOICE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Not all voice is periodic (harmonic). Aperiodic noise components include:

ASPIRATION NOISE:
  Turbulent airflow through the glottis when folds don't fully close.
  Creates breathiness. Prominent at start of words beginning with /h/.

FRICATION NOISE:
  Turbulence created at constriction points in the vocal tract.
  Examples: /s/, /f/, /ʃ/ (sh), /θ/ (th) are pure fricative sounds.

SPECTRAL NOISE FLOOR:
  A general, distributed noise level across all frequencies.
  Higher noise floor = rougher, more disordered, or whispered voice.

HNR (Harmonic-to-Noise Ratio):
  A key voice quality metric.
  High HNR (>20 dB) = clean, healthy voice
  Low HNR (<10 dB)  = rough, breathy, or pathological voice

PART III — CLASSIFICATION SYSTEMS: ORGANIZING THE VOICE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.1 CLASSIFICATION BY RANGE (Singing/Musical Tradition)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MALE VOICES:
  Bass         : Lowest male voice. Range: E2–E4. (e.g., Barry White)
  Bass-Baritone : Between bass and baritone.
  Baritone     : Most common male voice. Range: A2–A4. (e.g., Tom Waits)
  Tenor        : High male voice. Range: C3–C5. (e.g., Pavarotti)
  Countertenor : Uses falsetto primarily. Range overlaps female mezzo.
  
FEMALE VOICES:
  Contralto    : Lowest female voice. Range: E3–E5.
  Mezzo-soprano: Middle female voice. Range: A3–A5.
  Soprano      : Highest female voice. Range: C4–C6. (e.g., Celine Dion)
  Coloratura soprano: Extremely agile high soprano; can reach C7 or beyond.
  
CHILDREN'S VOICES:
  Treble       : Pre-pubescent, no distinction between male/female.

NOTE: These classifications apply to trained singing voices.
Spoken voice ranges are considerably narrower in practice.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.2 CLASSIFICATION BY ACOUSTIC/PERCEPTUAL QUALITY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

BREATHINESS:
  Caused by incomplete glottal closure. Air escapes during phonation.
  Results in audible aspiration mixed with voice. Perception: airy, soft, sexy,
  weak, or sultry depending on context and degree.
  Examples: Marilyn Monroe, ASMR voices.

ROUGHNESS / CREAKINESS:
  Caused by irregular vocal fold vibration, tension, or lesions.
  Sounds scratchy, gravelly, raspy. Often associated with personality (edgy,
  authoritative, aged).
  Examples: Tom Waits, Leonard Cohen.

NASALITY:
  How much the nasal cavity resonates during speech.
  Types:
  - Hypernasality   : Too much nasal resonance (velopharyngeal insufficiency)
  - Hyponasality    : Too little nasal resonance (blocked nasal cavity, cold)
  - Mixed nasality  : Both present

PRESSED/TENSE VOICE:
  Overly tight glottal closure; high subglottal pressure.
  Sounds strained, hard, or aggressive.

RESONANCE CHARACTER:
  - Oral resonance : "Forward," bright sound
  - Pharyngeal     : "Dark," deep, back-of-throat sound
  - Nasal          : Added nasal resonance color
  - Chest          : Heavy, warm, rumbling quality

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.3 CLASSIFICATION BY PHONETIC CATEGORY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Speech sounds are systematically classified in PHONETICS.

CONSONANTS — classified by three axes:

  [1] VOICING:
      Voiced   = vocal folds vibrating during the sound (e.g., /b/, /d/, /g/)
      Voiceless= vocal folds not vibrating (e.g., /p/, /t/, /k/)

  [2] PLACE OF ARTICULATION (where in the mouth the constriction happens):
      Bilabial     : Both lips    → /p/, /b/, /m/
      Labiodental  : Lip + teeth  → /f/, /v/
      Dental       : Tongue + teeth → /θ/ (think), /ð/ (this)
      Alveolar     : Tongue + alveolar ridge → /t/, /d/, /n/, /s/, /z/
      Postalveolar : Behind alveolar ridge → /ʃ/ (sh), /ʒ/ (vision)
      Palatal      : Tongue + hard palate → /j/ (yes)
      Velar        : Tongue + soft palate → /k/, /g/, /ŋ/ (ring)
      Glottal      : At the larynx → /h/, glottal stop /ʔ/
      Uvular       : Tongue + uvula → /R/ (French r)
      Pharyngeal   : Tongue root + pharynx → Arabic /ʕ/

  [3] MANNER OF ARTICULATION (how the airflow is modulated):
      Plosive/Stop  : Complete blockage then release → /p/, /t/, /k/, /b/, /d/, /g/
      Fricative     : Narrow constriction causing turbulence → /f/, /s/, /ʃ/
      Affricate     : Stop + fricative combined → /tʃ/ (ch), /dʒ/ (j)
      Nasal         : Air through nasal cavity → /m/, /n/, /ŋ/
      Lateral       : Air around sides of tongue → /l/
      Approximant   : Near-contact, smooth flow → /w/, /j/, /r/
      Trill/Flap    : Rapid contact → rolled /r/ (Italian, Spanish)

VOWELS — classified by three axes:

  [1] TONGUE HEIGHT    : High → /i/ (ee), /u/ (oo) | Low → /a/ (ah)
  [2] TONGUE BACKNESS  : Front → /i/, /e/ | Central → /ə/ (schwa) | Back → /u/, /o/
  [3] LIP ROUNDING     : Rounded → /u/, /o/ | Unrounded → /i/, /a/

  The VOWEL QUADRILATERAL is a standard diagram showing vowel positions.

SUPRASEGMENTALS (properties spanning multiple sounds):
  Prosody    : The overall "melody" of speech
  Stress     : Which syllables are emphasized
  Intonation : The rise and fall of pitch across an utterance
  Tone       : In tonal languages (Chinese, Thai), pitch distinguishes meaning
  Rhythm     : Pattern of stressed/unstressed syllables over time

PART IV — SPEECH VARIATIONS: SPEED, TONE, ACCENT, VOLUME & MORE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.1 SPEECH RATE (Speed)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MEASUREMENT:
  - Syllables per second (SPS): Most precise measure
  - Words per minute (WPM): Common in applied contexts
  - Articulation rate vs. speaking rate (speaking rate counts pauses)

TYPICAL VALUES:
  Slow speech      : <3 SPS  / <120 WPM
  Conversational   : 4–6 SPS / 130–180 WPM
  Fast speech      : >7 SPS  / >200 WPM
  Auctioneers/rapid speech: Up to 300+ WPM with reduced clarity

VARIABLES AFFECTING SPEED:
  - Language    : Finnish, Japanese tend to be fast (syllable-timed)
                  German, English tend toward stress-timed rhythm
  - Emotion     : Anxiety/excitement → faster; depression/fatigue → slower
  - Formality   : Formal → slower, clearer; casual → faster, reduced
  - Age         : Children and elderly speak slower on average
  - Clarity intent: Talking to non-native speakers → deliberate slowing

CONNECTED SPEECH PROCESSES (what happens at natural speed):
  Assimilation  : A sound changes to match adjacent sound
                  ("ten boys" → "tem boys")
  Elision       : A sound disappears entirely
                  ("next day" → "nex day")
  Liaison/Linking: Sounds blend across word boundaries
                  ("an apple" → "annapple")
  Reduction     : Unstressed sounds weakened
                  ("and" → "en," "to" → "te," "you" → "ye")
  Intrusion     : Extra sound inserted
                  ("law and order" → "lawr and order")

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.2 PITCH AND TONE VARIATIONS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PITCH RANGE:
  The span a speaker habitually uses.
  Wide range = more expressive, emotionally engaged, charismatic
  Narrow range = monotone, reserved, possibly depressed or authoritative

PITCH LEVEL:
  The baseline (habitual) pitch around which variation occurs.
  Determined by biology (fold size) but modifiable by habit or training.
  Habitual pitch often deviates from natural optimal pitch.

INTONATION PATTERNS:

  Declarative (statement) : Typically falls at end (falling intonation)
                            "I went to the store." (↘)

  Question (yes/no)       : Typically rises at end (rising intonation)
                            "Did you go to the store?" (↗)

  Question (WH-)          : Often falls
                            "Where did you go?" (↘)

  List intonation         : Rising on each item except last
                            "I bought apples(↗), bananas(↗), and oranges(↘)."

  High Rise Terminal (HRT): Using rising intonation on statements
                            (Australian/New Zealand; also young speakers)
                            Makes statements sound like questions.

PROSODIC STRESS:
  Changing which word receives pitch/loudness emphasis changes meaning:
  "I didn't say HE stole it." (someone else did)
  "I didn't say he STOLE it." (he did something else)
  "I didn't SAY he stole it." (I implied it)

EMOTIONAL PITCH PATTERNS:
  Anger    : High pitch, wide range, abrupt changes, loud
  Sadness  : Low pitch, narrow range, slow, quiet
  Fear     : High pitch, fast rate, tremor
  Joy      : High pitch, wide range, rapid
  Boredom  : Low pitch, monotone, slow

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.3 BREATHINESS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DEFINITION:
  A perceptual quality created by audible air turbulence mixed with phonation.
  The degree of breathiness exists on a spectrum from fully phonated to whisper.

ACOUSTIC CORRELATES:
  - High H1-H2 ratio
  - Low HNR (high noise floor)
  - Increased spectral tilt (high frequencies less energetic)
  - Audible aspiration noise

CAUSES:
  Habitual: Some speakers consistently use breathy phonation as a style,
            often associated with femininity, sensuality, or relaxation.
  Anatomical: Certain laryngeal shapes allow more air escape.
  Paralysis: Unilateral vocal fold paralysis → constant breathiness.
  Fatigue: Tired or strained voice becomes breathier.

EXTREME CASE — WHISPER:
  Complete cessation of vocal fold vibration.
  Glottis opens into a triangular or slit shape.
  All sound is produced by turbulent airflow, no periodic phonation.
  Whisper can still convey lexical content but loses prosodic information.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.4 VOLUME (Loudness)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PHYSICAL BASIS:
  Loudness is primarily controlled by subglottal air pressure.
  Higher air pressure → larger fold displacement → greater sound amplitude.

  Secondary factors:
  - Vocal fold closure completeness (tighter = louder)
  - Resonance optimization (a well-tuned vocal tract amplifies efficiently)

DYNAMIC RANGE:
  A trained professional singer can produce ~30 dB of dynamic variation
  (from pianissimo to fortissimo) on a single pitch.
  Most untrained speakers use only ~10–15 dB of variation.

INTENSITY-FREQUENCY INTERACTION:
  Loudness and pitch are not independent.
  Singing/speaking louder tends to raise pitch slightly (Bernoulli effect).
  Careful training decouples them.

SOCIAL/CONTEXTUAL VOLUME CONTROL:
  Intimate conversation  : ~45–55 dB (half-meter distance)
  Normal conversation    : ~55–65 dB (1–2 meter distance)
  Group conversation     : ~65–70 dB
  Public speech (unaided): ~70–80 dB
  Performance/stage      : ~80–90 dB projected

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.5 ACCENTS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

An accent is a systematic pattern of phonological, prosodic, and sometimes
lexical and grammatical features that marks a speaker as belonging to a
particular geographic, social, or ethnic group.

DIMENSIONS OF ACCENT:

  [A] PHONEMIC DIFFERENCES (Which sounds are used):
      - Presence or absence of the "rhotic" /r/ (American vs British RP)
      - Trap-Bath split: Short /a/ in "bath" vs long /aː/ (Northern vs Southern UK)
      - Cot-caught merger: Different vowels vs. same vowel (American regional)
      - Pin-pen merger: Merging /ɪ/ and /ɛ/ before nasals (American South)

  [B] ALLOPHONIC DIFFERENCES (How sounds are realized):
      - T-flapping: /t/ → [ɾ] between vowels in American English ("butter" → "budder")
      - Glottalization: /t/ → glottal stop /ʔ/ in Cockney ("bottle" → "bo'le")
      - Vowel shift: The Northern Cities Vowel Shift (Chicago, Detroit, Cleveland)

  [C] PROSODIC DIFFERENCES:
      - Stress placement patterns
      - Intonation contours (Irish English has characteristic high-rising pitch)
      - Rhythm: French is more syllable-timed; English is stress-timed

  [D] VOICE QUALITY DIFFERENCES:
      Some accents correlate with habitual voice quality settings:
      - Liverpool/Scouse: Often nasal, forward resonance
      - Yorkshire: Often darker, back resonance
      - Southern American: Often drawling, relaxed larynx

ACCENT CLASSIFICATION (English examples):

  RECEIVED PRONUNCIATION (RP): Prestige British; non-rhotic; posh
  GENERAL AMERICAN (GenAm): Rhotic; neutral baseline American
  COCKNEY: East London; non-rhotic; heavy glottalization; h-dropping
  AUSTRALIAN: Non-rhotic; raised vowels; rising intonation patterns
  SCOUSE (Liverpool): Rhotic at times; nasalized; unique /k/ pronunciation
  GEORDIE (Newcastle): Preserved older vowels; distinct from standard British
  SCOTS: Rhotic; Scottish vowel length rule; distinct /x/ (loch)
  SOUTHERN AMERICAN: Monophthong drawl; pin-pen merger; vowel shifts
  NEW YORK: Non-rhotic historically; raised THOUGHT vowel; tense-lax vowels
  AFRICAN AMERICAN VERNACULAR ENGLISH (AAVE): Distinct prosody; vowel shifts;
    systematic grammatical features (copula deletion, aspect marking)

SECOND LANGUAGE ACCENTS:
  When learning a new language, speakers carry over phonological patterns
  from their native language. These patterns are systematic and predictable
  based on the phonological inventory of the L1 (first language).

  French accent in English: No /h/; nasalized vowels; different rhythm
  Japanese accent in English: CV (consonant-vowel) syllable structure; no /r/-/l/ distinction
  German accent in English: Devoicing final consonants; uvular /R/
  Spanish accent in English: Vowel reduction absent; /v/ → /b/ confusion

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.6 STUTTERING (Dysfluency)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DEFINITION:
  Stuttering is a fluency disorder characterized by involuntary disruptions
  to the forward flow of speech.

TYPES OF STUTTERING MOMENTS:
  Repetitions     : Repetition of sounds, syllables, or words
                    "I-I-I want a c-c-cookie."
  Prolongations   : Stretching a sound
                    "Sssssso I went to the store."
  Blocks          : Silent, frozen cessation of airflow/phonation
                    Mouth positioned but no sound produced.
  Revisions       : Restarting a word or phrase differently.
  Interjections   : Filler sounds while stuck ("um," "uh," "like").

SECONDARY BEHAVIORS (learned coping):
  Eye blinks, head jerks, facial grimaces, hand tapping, word avoidance,
  circumlocution (talking around feared words), avoidance of phone calls.

PREVALENCE:
  ~1% of adults; ~5% of children stutter at some point.
  Male:Female ratio roughly 3:1 to 4:1 in persistent stuttering.

NEUROLOGICAL BASIS:
  - Differences in timing of basal ganglia and cerebellar activity
  - Reduced activation of Broca's area (speech production)
  - Disrupted auditory-motor integration
  - Genetic component: 60–80% heritability estimated

SITUATIONAL VARIATION:
  Stuttering fluctuates dramatically with situation:
  - Worse under stress, time pressure, unfamiliar audience, phone calls
  - Better when singing, whispering, speaking in unison, reading aloud alone
  - This variability is itself diagnostically significant

CLUTTERING (distinct from stuttering):
  Excessively rapid, irregular, collapsed speech.
  Omission of syllables, merging words, erratic rhythm.
  Often accompanied by disorganized language.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.7 SLANGS, DIALECTS AND SOCIOLINGUISTIC VARIATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLANG:
  Informal vocabulary, often group-specific, rapidly changing.
  Functions: In-group bonding, identity marking, opacity from outsiders.
  Categories:
  - Youth slang: Changes generation-to-generation
  - Professional slang (jargon): Medical, military, legal, criminal
  - Regional slang: Tied to geographic communities
  - Internet/digital slang: "lowkey," "slay," "no cap," "bussin"

DIALECT:
  A variety of a language differing in grammar, vocabulary, and pronunciation.
  Distinguished from accent: dialect includes grammar; accent is only phonology.
  
  All speakers have a dialect; there is no "dialectless" speech.
  "Standard" dialects have social prestige but no inherent linguistic superiority.

CODE-SWITCHING:
  The ability to shift between dialects, registers, or languages depending
  on social context. Common in bilingual communities and across social strata.
  Example: Speaking AAVE at home, "Standard American English" at work.

REGISTER:
  A stylistic variety of language used in a specific social context.
  Formal register    : Academic writing, professional speech
  Consultative       : Standard conversation, professional interaction
  Casual register    : Friends, family
  Intimate register  : Partners, very close family
  Frozen register    : Fixed texts (prayers, pledges, legal formulas)

IDIOLECT:
  The unique, individual language variety of one specific person.
  Includes their personal vocabulary choices, pronunciation habits, rhythm,
  pitch patterns, and every other linguistic feature that identifies them.
  The idiolect is the ultimate individual fingerprint of voice.

PART V — BEHAVIORAL & PSYCHOLOGICAL VOCAL PATTERNS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5.1 VOCAL HABITS AND GIMMICKS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Vocal habits are recurring, often unconscious behavioral patterns in speech.
They are not disorders but stylistic or habitual tendencies.

FILLER WORDS / HESITATION PHENOMENA:
  "Um," "uh," "er," "like," "you know," "right," "I mean," "basically"
  
  Functions:
  - Hold the conversational floor while planning next utterance
  - Signal that the speaker has more to say
  - Social bonding / alignment markers ("you know")
  - Stylistic markers of casual speech

  Some individuals have characteristic filler preferences that identify them.
  "Like" is associated with younger speakers and California English.
  "Right" is common in British and Australian speech.
  "Basically" marks oversimplification or the opening of an explanation.

VOCAL FRY (Creaky Voice / Glottal Fry):
  Irregular, low-frequency vibration; sounds like creaky door or popcorn popping.
  Used habitually especially at the ends of phrases.
  Associated with certain American young women's speech in media.
  Normal feature of many languages (Danish, for example, uses it systematically).

UPSPEAK / HIGH RISING TERMINAL:
  Ending statements with rising intonation (as if asking a question).
  "So I went to the store? And I bought some milk?"
  Associated with Australian and New Zealand English, young American speakers.
  Pragmatic functions: checking comprehension, maintaining interest.

UPTALK REDUCTION:
  Opposite tendency; extremely flat, monotone delivery.
  Can signal authority, certainty, or emotional flatness.

TRAILING OFF:
  Ending utterances at decreasing volume or dropping the final word.
  
OVER-ARTICULATION:
  Excessive clarity, exaggerated precision, sometimes condescending.
  Used for emphasis, to non-native speakers, by actors.

UNDER-ARTICULATION:
  Slurred, reduced, swallowed speech. Casual style; can signal intimacy.

VOCAL GIMMICKS (individual signature sounds):
  Specific individuals develop characteristic vocal signatures:
  - A characteristic laugh pattern
  - A habitual throat clear before speaking
  - A sharp intake of breath at certain moments
  - A click sound when starting a thought
  - A particular way of stretching certain vowels
  - Humming agreement sounds ("mm-hmm") with a specific melody

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5.2 EMOTIONAL VOICE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Emotion dramatically alters the voice. Key mechanisms:

PHYSIOLOGICAL PATHWAYS:
  - Sympathetic nervous system (fight-or-flight):
    → Tightens laryngeal muscles → higher pitch
    → Increased respiration rate → more air available
    → Dry mouth (reduced saliva) → affects resonance
    → Muscle tremor → vocal tremor/quaver

  - Parasympathetic system (calm/relaxed):
    → Relaxed laryngeal muscles → lower pitch possible
    → Regular breathing → steady subglottal pressure

EMOTION → ACOUSTIC MAPPING:

  ANGER       : F0 high, wide range; loud; fast rate; abrupt onsets; harsh quality
  FEAR        : F0 high; irregular; tremor; faster rate; less resonance
  SADNESS     : F0 low; narrow range; slow rate; quiet; longer pauses; breathier
  JOY/ELATION : F0 high; wide range; fast rate; louder; more pitch variation
  DISGUST     : Low F0; descending contours; reduced opening; constricted quality
  SURPRISE    : Abrupt F0 jump; wide range; loud; fast
  CONTEMPT    : One-sided, constricted; lower pitch; clipped or drawling
  LOVE/INTIMACY: Soft; low to medium F0; breathy; slow; gentle onsets

MICRO-EXPRESSIONS IN VOICE:
  Short, involuntary moments of emotional leakage, even when deliberately masked.
  Detectable through very fine spectral analysis or perceptual training.
  Used in forensic phonetics and lie detection research.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5.3 OCD AND COMPULSIVE VOCAL PATTERNS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

OCD (Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder) can manifest in speech in various ways:

INTRUSIVE SPEECH PATTERNS:
  - Repetition compulsions: Replaying or re-saying words to "get them right"
  - Counting syllables or words mentally before speaking
  - Avoiding certain words/numbers/sounds for magical-thinking reasons
  - Echolalia-like repetitions tied to intrusive thoughts

SCRUPULOSITY IN SPEECH:
  Excessive need for precision in word choice; constant self-correction;
  meta-commentary on own speech ("I mean," "or rather," "what I really mean is").

TICS (TOURETTE SYNDROME — distinct from OCD but co-occurring):
  Motor and vocal tics:
  - Simple vocal tics: Throat clearing, sniffing, grunting, squeaking
  - Complex vocal tics: Words, phrases, echolalia (repeating others),
    palilalia (repeating own last word/phrase), coprolalia (rare; involuntary
    offensive words; present in only ~10–15% of Tourette cases)
  
  Tics are involuntary, semi-voluntary, or voluntarily suppressible;
  suppression leads to building tension and eventual release.

ANXIOUS SPEECH PATTERNS:
  Rapid speech (cognitive rushing), frequent false starts, excessive
  qualifications, upward pitch shifts, irregular breathing, voice breaks.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5.4 VOICE AND IDENTITY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The voice is deeply tied to personal identity:

GENDERED VOICE:
  - Average F0 is a primary cue to perceived gender
  - Beyond F0: resonance size, breathiness, intonation patterns, style
  - Transgender individuals often work to modify voice
    (F0, resonance, prosody all targeted in voice feminization/masculinization)

AGE-RELATED VOCAL CHANGES:
  - Children: High F0, irregular, developing coordination
  - Adolescence: Rapid deepening (especially male voice: "voice breaking")
  - Adulthood: Stable period
  - Aging (50+): Increased breathiness; higher F0 in males ("presbyphonia");
                  reduced range; slower rate; slight tremor common

PROFESSIONAL VOICE IDENTITY:
  Some professions develop highly consistent, recognizable voice profiles:
  - Radio/TV presenters: Smooth, controlled, warm resonance
  - Lawyers/professors: Deliberate pacing; authority prosody
  - Nurses/therapists: Soft, even, calming patterns
  - Military/police: Clipped, low, commanding
  - Actors: Extreme versatility; trained to shift entirely

VOCAL FINGERPRINT:
  Every individual's voice has unique acoustic features analogous to a fingerprint.
  Speaker identification is a field combining phonetics, acoustic analysis,
  and machine learning (used in forensics and biometric authentication).

PART VI — PATHOLOGICAL & ATYPICAL VOICES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6.1 VOICE DISORDERS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ORGANIC DISORDERS (structural changes):
  Vocal fold nodules  : Callus-like lesions from vocal abuse; reduce closure;
                        cause breathiness and roughness
  Vocal fold polyps   : Fluid-filled lesions; often asymmetric; cause roughness
  Vocal fold cysts    : Benign mass; can cause diplophonia (double voice)
  Laryngeal cancer    : Severe dysphonia; requires medical treatment
  Recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy: Unilateral fold paralysis; breathy, weak voice

FUNCTIONAL DISORDERS (no structural change):
  Muscle tension dysphonia (MTD): Excessive laryngeal muscle tension;
                                   strained, effortful voice; pain possible
  Puberphonia (mutational falsetto): Post-pubertal male retains falsetto;
                                     voice not lowered despite anatomy
  Psychogenic aphonia: Complete loss of voice without structural cause;
                        often related to psychological trauma or extreme stress

NEUROLOGICAL VOICE DISORDERS:
  Spasmodic dysphonia : Involuntary spasms of laryngeal muscles;
                        Adductor type → strained, strangled quality
                        Abductor type → breathy, whispery breaks
  Essential tremor    : Regular oscillation in pitch; shaking voice
  Parkinson's         : Hypophonia (too quiet); monotone; limited range
  ALS                 : Progressive dysarthria; mixed quality changes
  
APRAXIA OF SPEECH:
  Inability to plan/program the motor sequences for speech.
  Inconsistent errors; better automatic speech than voluntary.

DYSARTHRIA:
  Motor speech disorder from muscle weakness/paralysis/incoordination.
  Seven types (flaccid, spastic, ataxic, hypokinetic, hyperkinetic,
  unilateral UMN, mixed) each with distinct voice and speech characteristics.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6.2 MODIFIED AND EXTREME VOICE USES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

BEATBOXING:
  Oral mimicry of percussion instruments using the vocal tract.
  Involves: glottal stops, bilabial trills, various ingressives (sounds
  made on inhaled air), tight lip buzz, tongue clicks, etc.
  Physiologically unusual: produces sounds that don't occur in natural speech.

THROAT SINGING (Overtone Singing / Khoomei):
  Technique (from Tuvan, Mongolian tradition) where a single singer
  simultaneously produces a drone pitch and a melody in the overtones.
  Achieved by extreme shaping of the vocal tract to isolate specific harmonics.

VOCAL DISTORTION (Metal/Rock):
  Techniques to create distorted, growled, or screamed tones:
  - False cord distortion: Aryepiglottic folds vibrate over or alongside folds
  - Fry scream: Extreme vocal fry at high volume
  - Subharmonic singing: Complex fold interaction producing sub-octave pitches

TRAINED VENTRILOQUISM:
  Speaking without visible lip movement.
  Substitutes labial sounds (b, p, m) with back-of-mouth alternatives (g, d, n).
  "Better" → "Gedder"; "Pepsi" → "Degsy"
  Relies on audience illusion and misdirection.

VOICE ACTING AND MIMICRY:
  Professional voice actors control:
  - Pitch baseline and range
  - Resonance (front/back, high/low)
  - Breathiness level
  - Speech rate
  - Accent and phoneme substitutions
  - Emotional overlay
  - Idiosyncratic habits of the target voice

PART VII — PRAGMATIC FRAMEWORK
         "IF I WANT TO UNDERSTAND AND ARTIFICIALLY REPRODUCE VOICES, HOW DO I?"
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

This section answers the central practical question: how do you go from raw
understanding of voice to intentional, structured reproduction — whether for
AI synthesis, acting, voice acting, audio design, or forensic analysis?

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
7.1 THE REPRODUCTION PROBLEM: LAYERS OF VOICE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

To reproduce a voice, you must reproduce it at EVERY layer simultaneously.
Voice is not one thing — it is a stack of nested systems:

  LAYER 1 — PHONETIC LAYER (What sounds are used)
  LAYER 2 — PROSODIC LAYER (How rhythm, pitch, stress are patterned)
  LAYER 3 — VOICE QUALITY LAYER (The "color" of the voice)
  LAYER 4 — BEHAVIORAL/HABITUAL LAYER (Fillers, gimmicks, habits)
  LAYER 5 — CONTEXTUAL/EMOTIONAL LAYER (Variation with situation)
  LAYER 6 — IDIOLECTAL LAYER (The personal vocabulary, expressions)

  Missing any layer = incomplete reproduction.
  Most cheap TTS systems fail at layers 2–6.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
7.2 STEP-BY-STEP ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STEP 1: ACOUSTIC ANALYSIS
──────────────────────────

Gather recordings. Analyze with acoustic tools (Praat, Audacity, openSMILE).

Measure:
  a) Fundamental frequency (F0)
     - Mean F0 (average pitch)
     - F0 range (lowest to highest used)
     - F0 variability (SD of F0 across utterances)
     - F0 contour patterns (typical shapes over phrases)

  b) Intensity
     - Mean intensity (dB)
     - Dynamic range (dB variation)
     - Intensity-F0 coupling (does pitch rise when louder?)

  c) Voice quality metrics
     - HNR (breathiness/roughness indicator)
     - Jitter (cycle-to-cycle frequency variation; <0.5% = healthy)
     - Shimmer (cycle-to-cycle amplitude variation; <3 dB = healthy)
     - CPP (Cepstral Peak Prominence; higher = cleaner voice)
     - H1-H2 difference (register, breathiness)

  d) Spectral characteristics
     - Formant frequencies (F1–F4)
     - Spectral tilt (slope of harmonic energy across frequency)
     - Overall spectral envelope shape

  e) Temporal characteristics
     - Speaking rate (syllables/second)
     - Articulation rate (rate excluding pauses)
     - Pause duration and frequency
     - Phone-level durations (which sounds are stretched/reduced)


STEP 2: PHONOLOGICAL/ACCENT ANALYSIS
──────────────────────────────────────

Transcribe speech using the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA).

Identify:
  a) Accent features
     - Rhotic or non-rhotic?
     - Which vowel mergers are present?
     - Which consonants are modified (T-flapping, glottalization, etc.)?
     - Systematic phoneme substitutions

  b) Allophonic rules
     - How does each phoneme actually sound in context?
     - Are there unusual pronunciations of specific words?

  c) Phonotactic preferences
     - Does the speaker prefer certain consonant clusters?
     - Are there sounds they consistently avoid or replace?

  d) Word-specific pronunciations
     - Idiosyncratic pronunciations of common words
     - Stress placement on multisyllabic words


STEP 3: PROSODIC ANALYSIS
───────────────────────────

  a) Intonation patterns
     - Do statements end falling or rising?
     - How dramatic are intonation movements?
     - Characteristic pitch shapes on questions, exclamations, lists

  b) Stress and rhythm
     - Does the speaker strongly differentiate stressed/unstressed syllables?
     - Are they syllable-timed or stress-timed?
     - Are there characteristic rhythmic "fingerprints"?

  c) Phrasing
     - Where do phrase boundaries fall?
     - Are pauses long, short, filled (um/uh) or silent?
     - Does the speaker use pause for emphasis or just hesitation?

  d) Tempo
     - Overall speaking rate
     - Is rate consistent or does it accelerate/decelerate situationally?
     - Does the speaker slow for emphasis or rush through certain content?


STEP 4: VOICE QUALITY PROFILING
─────────────────────────────────

Create a voice quality profile. For each dimension, rate on a scale:

  PITCH LEVEL      : Very Low — Low — Medium — High — Very High
  BREATHINESS      : Clean — Slightly Breathy — Moderately Breathy — Very Breathy — Whisper
  ROUGHNESS        : Smooth — Slightly Rough — Rough — Very Rough
  RESONANCE        : Very Front — Front — Neutral — Back — Very Back (Dark)
  NASALITY         : Hyponasal — Normal — Slightly Nasal — Very Nasal — Hypernasal
  TENSION          : Relaxed — Neutral — Slightly Tense — Tense — Strained
  LOUDNESS         : Very Soft — Soft — Moderate — Loud — Very Loud
  REGISTER         : Pure Chest — Mix — Pure Head — Falsetto — Fry

  This profile is similar to CAPE-V (Consensus Auditory-Perceptual Evaluation
  of Voice) used in clinical speech-language pathology.


STEP 5: BEHAVIORAL/HABITUAL INVENTORY
──────────────────────────────────────

Document every recurring idiosyncrasy:

  a) Filler inventory
     - Which fillers does the speaker use?
     - How frequently? (per minute, per turn)
     - In what contexts? (thinking, hesitating, stalling)

  b) Gimmick list
     - Any characteristic laugh?
     - Any habitual interjections or verbal tics?
     - Any characteristic phrase openings or closings?
     - Any rhythmic patterns or verbal "signature" phrases

  c) Error patterns
     - Any systematic mispronunciations?
     - Any repeated grammatical structures?
     - Any word-finding patterns (do they circle before hitting the word)?

  d) Emotional voice signature
     - How does the voice change when excited? When nervous? When confident?
     - Are transitions smooth or abrupt?


STEP 6: CONTEXTUAL VARIATION MAPPING
──────────────────────────────────────

No voice is static. Map how the voice changes:

  Context            → Expected variation
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────
  Formal setting     → Cleaner articulation, slower, lower pitch variation
  Casual/friendly    → Faster, more reduced speech, warmer quality
  Telephone          → Often more careful enunciation; louder
  Whispered/private  → Aperiodic, intimate, slower
  Excited/passionate → Faster, higher F0, wider range
  Tired/fatigued     → Breathier, lower, slower, more monotone
  Under stress       → Higher F0, tighter quality, faster or fragmented


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
7.3 ARTIFICIAL REPRODUCTION — TECHNICAL APPROACHES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Once analysis is complete, reproduction can be approached in several ways:

─────────────────────────────────────────
A) SPEECH SYNTHESIS (Text-to-Speech / TTS)
─────────────────────────────────────────

GENERATION 1 — FORMANT SYNTHESIS (1950s–1980s):
  Directly controls formant frequencies and bandwidths.
  Rule-based, fully parameterized.
  Completely intelligible but robotic (classic "computer voice").
  No need for recordings; all acoustic properties specified manually.
  DECTalk (used by Stephen Hawking) was this approach.

GENERATION 2 — CONCATENATIVE SYNTHESIS (1990s–2000s):
  Cuts and recombines recorded speech units (phones, diphones, triphones).
  More natural sounding but limited to recorded speaker's inventory.
  Requires large recorded corpus of the target voice.
  Requires careful inventory collection: all phoneme combinations needed.
  Unit selection: System picks best-fitting unit from corpus.
  Prosody modification via time-stretching and pitch-shifting algorithms.

GENERATION 3 — STATISTICAL PARAMETRIC SYNTHESIS (HMM/GMM era, 2000s–2015):
  Uses Hidden Markov Models to learn acoustic parameters.
  Can generate voice from relatively small corpus.
  More flexible than concatenative but slightly more muffled quality.
  Key parameters: F0, spectrum (mel cepstral coefficients), duration.

GENERATION 4 — NEURAL TTS (2016–present):
  Deep learning models learn voice synthesis end-to-end.
  
  Key milestones:
  - WaveNet (DeepMind, 2016): Autoregressive neural waveform generation;
    first truly natural-sounding synthetic speech
  - Tacotron (Google, 2017): Text → spectrogram (then vocoder generates audio)
  - Tacotron 2 (2018): Tacotron + WaveNet; near-human quality
  - FastSpeech / VITS (2020–2022): Non-autoregressive; real-time synthesis
  - VALL-E (Microsoft, 2023): Zero-shot voice cloning from 3 seconds of audio
  - Tortoise TTS, ElevenLabs, etc.: Commercial applications

  Neural models learn everything from data: prosody, voice quality, accents.
  Given enough target audio, they can clone specific voices.

VOICE CLONING PIPELINE:
  1. Collect target audio (10 seconds to hours depending on method)
  2. Train speaker encoder (learn speaker's acoustic embedding vector)
  3. Condition synthesis model on this embedding
  4. Generate speech in target voice from any input text

─────────────────────────────────────────
B) VOICE CONVERSION
─────────────────────────────────────────

Rather than synthesizing from scratch, voice conversion transforms one voice
into another while preserving the linguistic content.

Pipeline:
  Source audio → Feature extraction → Mapping → Feature resynthesis → Target audio

Components:
  - Spectral conversion: Maps spectral envelope of source to target
  - F0 conversion: Transforms pitch statistics to match target
  - Duration/rate conversion: Modifies timing to match target
  - Voice quality: Maps breathiness, roughness, resonance

Modern neural approaches (StarGANv2-VC, AutoVC, VQMIVC) can do this
without parallel recordings.

─────────────────────────────────────────
C) PROSODY MODELING
─────────────────────────────────────────

Even perfect segmental quality fails without correct prosody.

Approaches:
  Rule-based prosody: ToBI labeling system; explicit rules for intonation
  Statistical prosody: HMM/CRF models predicting F0 contours from text
  Neural prosody: Attention mechanisms in sequence models learn prosody
  Hierarchical models: Sentence → phrase → word → syllable → phone level

For accent-specific prosody:
  Models need training data from native speakers of target accent.
  Transfer learning: Fine-tune general model on small accent-specific corpus.

─────────────────────────────────────────
D) BEHAVIORAL LAYER (Fillers, Disfluencies, Habits)
─────────────────────────────────────────

This is the most underrepresented layer in TTS systems.
Clean TTS sounds "too clean" for human conversation simulation.

Strategies:
  - Insert filler rules: Probabilistically insert "um," "uh" at phrase boundaries
  - Disfluency modeling: Train on spontaneous (not read) speech corpora
  - Pause insertion: Model turn-taking and thinking pauses
  - Vocal fry insertion: Add vocal fry at phrase endings programmatically
  - Habit injection: Detect specific contexts and insert known habits
  - Emotional variation: Multi-style TTS with emotion conditioning

Spontaneous speech corpora for training:
  - Switchboard (English telephone conversations)
  - CALLHOME
  - Buckeye Corpus (conversational American English, phonetically detailed)
  - AESOP, DiPCo, AMI corpus (meeting speech)

─────────────────────────────────────────
E) HUMAN REPRODUCTION (Acting / Voice Acting)
─────────────────────────────────────────

For a human attempting to reproduce a voice:

  Step 1: AUDITORY ANALYSIS (perceptual training)
    Train your ear. Listen to the target voice extensively.
    Identify pitch level, quality, accent, rate, habits.

  Step 2: PHONOLOGICAL MAPPING
    Transcribe how the target person pronounces key words.
    Map systematic substitutions.
    Practice individual phoneme changes in isolation, then in words.

  Step 3: RESONANCE PLACEMENT
    Physically adjust vocal tract shape to approximate target resonance.
    Experiment with lip rounding, tongue position, jaw tension, larynx height.
    Higher larynx → brighter, more forward resonance (feminine-coded)
    Lower larynx → darker, deeper resonance (masculine-coded)

  Step 4: PITCH AND RHYTHM
    Match mean F0 of target.
    Match intonation contour shapes.
    Match rhythm and rate.

  Step 5: BEHAVIORAL LAYER
    Practice their characteristic fillers, laughs, habits.
    These are often the most immediately recognizable features.

  Step 6: CONTEXTUAL TESTING
    Perform in varied contexts (excited, calm, explaining, questioning).
    A real voice shifts — practice those shifts, not just one mode.


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
7.4 STRUCTURED REPRODUCTION PLAN: THE COMPLETE VOICE MODEL
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Below is a structured plan combining all above elements into a unified
framework for voice analysis and reproduction.

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                    THE VOICE REPRODUCTION BLUEPRINT                      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PHASE 0: DATA COLLECTION
  ─────────────────────────
  □ Gather target voice recordings (varied contexts if possible)
  □ Include: read speech, spontaneous speech, emotional speech if available
  □ Aim for: high audio quality (>16kHz sample rate, low noise)
  □ Transcribe recordings orthographically and phonetically

  PHASE 1: ACOUSTIC PROFILING
  ─────────────────────────────
  □ Extract F0 statistics (mean, SD, range, typical contour shapes)
  □ Extract intensity statistics (mean, dynamic range)
  □ Extract voice quality metrics (HNR, jitter, shimmer, CPP)
  □ Extract H1-H2 ratio (breathiness indicator)
  □ Extract formant frequencies (F1–F4 for vowel system)
  □ Extract spectral tilt
  □ Extract speech rate (SPS, WPM, pause statistics)
  □ Extract phone duration statistics

  PHASE 2: PHONOLOGICAL PROFILING
  ──────────────────────────────────
  □ Identify accent group (geographic, social)
  □ Document vowel system (which vowels are merged, shifted, raised, lowered)
  □ Document consonant realizations (rhoticity, T-flapping, glottalization)
  □ Document allophonic rules
  □ Document any idiosyncratic pronunciations
  □ Map IPA phoneme system for this voice

  PHASE 3: PROSODIC PROFILING
  ─────────────────────────────
  □ Document intonation patterns (statement, question, list, emphasis)
  □ Document stress and rhythm type
  □ Document characteristic F0 shapes
  □ Document phrasing patterns
  □ Document tempo profile and tempo variation

  PHASE 4: VOICE QUALITY PROFILING
  ───────────────────────────────────
  □ Rate on CAPE-V dimensions (see Section 7.2 Step 4)
  □ Identify vocal register (chest, mixed, head, fry)
  □ Document resonance placement (front/back, high/low)
  □ Document nasality level

  PHASE 5: BEHAVIORAL PROFILING
  ───────────────────────────────
  □ Filler inventory with frequency counts
  □ Gimmick and tic inventory
  □ Emotional voice variation mapping
  □ Contextual shift mapping
  □ Idiolect lexical profiling (preferred words, phrases)

  PHASE 6: SYNTHESIS/REPRODUCTION
  ─────────────────────────────────
  
  FOR AI SYNTHESIS:
    □ Choose synthesis architecture (neural TTS, voice conversion, hybrid)
    □ Prepare training data from collected corpus
    □ Train speaker encoder or fine-tune base model
    □ Evaluate segmental quality (MOS, WER scores)
    □ Evaluate prosodic naturalness
    □ Evaluate voice quality match (perceptual + acoustic metrics)
    □ Inject behavioral layer (fillers, habits, disfluencies)
    □ Test across contexts and emotional registers
    □ Iterative refinement

  FOR HUMAN REPRODUCTION:
    □ Phonological mapping practice (systematic accent work)
    □ Resonance placement practice
    □ Pitch matching and intonation mimicry
    □ Rate and rhythm matching
    □ Behavioral layer drilling (fillers, gimmicks)
    □ Full contextual performance practice
    □ Blind listening test: Can others identify the target?

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
7.6 ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Voice reproduction raises serious ethical issues that must be addressed:

CONSENT:
  Cloning a real person's voice without their explicit consent is ethically
  problematic and in many jurisdictions is approaching or crossing legal lines.
  "Voice likeness" rights are being established in law (California, EU, others).

DEEPFAKES AND FRAUD:
  Voice cloning enables convincing audio deepfakes.
  Used in fraud: "grandparent scams," CEO fraud impersonation.
  Countermeasures: Voice authentication, deepfake detectors (ASVspoof).

CREATIVE/ARTISTIC USE:
  Recreating voices of historical figures, deceased people, or fictional
  characters requires careful judgment and transparency.

RESEARCH USE:
  Medical, forensic, and assistive technology research are primary
  legitimate applications.

ATTRIBUTION:
  Any artificially generated voice should be disclosed as synthetic.
  Standards emerging: watermarking of synthetic audio, disclosure laws.

  PRINCIPLE: Voice reproduction research and practice should default to
  transparency, consent, and non-harm. The technical capabilities described
  in this document carry responsibility proportional to their power.

PART VIII — MOOD AND ITS EFFECT ON VOICE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Mood is distinct from emotion. Emotion is acute, triggered, and short-lived.
Mood is a diffuse, sustained background state that colors ALL speech over
minutes, hours, or days. It is the baseline over which emotions are layered.
This distinction matters enormously for voice reproduction.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
8.1 MOOD vs. EMOTION: THE KEY DISTINCTION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  EMOTION:
    - Triggered by a specific event or stimulus
    - Intense, recognizable, short-lived (seconds to minutes)
    - Has a clear object: "I am angry AT you," "I am afraid OF that"
    - Produces sharp acoustic contrasts (sudden F0 jump, abrupt loudness shift)

  MOOD:
    - No necessary trigger; can be causeless or its cause forgotten
    - Moderate intensity, diffuse, sustained (hours to weeks)
    - Often has no specific object: "I just feel low today"
    - Produces gradual, global shifts in voice baseline
    - Often invisible to the speaker themselves

  IMPLICATION FOR VOICE:
    An angry emotion creates a spike. A depressed mood creates a persistent
    lowering of the entire vocal profile — pitch, energy, rate, richness.
    Reproducing a voice authentically requires capturing both layers.


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
8.2 MOOD STATES AND THEIR VOCAL SIGNATURES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DEPRESSED MOOD:
  One of the best-studied mood-voice relationships.

  Acoustic correlates:
  - F0 mean: Reduced (lower baseline pitch)
  - F0 range: Dramatically narrowed (loss of pitch variation → monotone)
  - F0 contour: Flatter, less modulated; falling at phrase ends
  - Speaking rate: Slower (cognitive and motor slowing)
  - Pause duration: Longer pauses; more frequent; longer response latency
  - Intensity: Reduced; dynamic range compressed
  - Voice quality: Increased breathiness; reduced resonance
  - Articulation: Reduced precision; "heavy" jaw; less facial muscle engagement
  - Prosody: Loss of linguistic stress contrast

  CLINICAL USE:
    F0 mean, pause duration, and energy measures are now used as
    objective biomarkers for depression severity and treatment tracking.
    Tools like AVEC (Audio/Visual Emotion Challenge) and Sonde Health
    use voice to passively monitor mental health.

  WHAT IT SOUNDS LIKE:
    The voice sounds tired, uninterested, effortful.
    Sentences trail off. Words are compressed. Silence expands.
    Even when the speaker is trying to sound "normal," the prosody
    betrays a leaden quality that trained listeners detect easily.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ANXIOUS/TENSE MOOD (chronic anxiety vs. acute fear):

  Acoustic correlates:
  - F0 mean: Elevated (chronic muscle tension raises baseline)
  - F0 range: Narrowed OR erratic (tension constrains natural modulation)
  - Speaking rate: Often faster; rushed; compressed pauses
  - Articulation: Over-precise or collapsed depending on coping strategy
  - Voice quality: Tighter, less resonant; pressed quality; thin timbre
  - Tremor: Mild, irregular F0 and amplitude fluctuation
  - Breathing: Shallower; more frequent breaths; audible inhalations
  - Filled pauses: More "um"s and "uh"s (cognitive load + hesitation)
  - Phrase length: Shorter utterances; more restarts

  IMPORTANT DISTINCTION:
    Anxious mood can produce either excessive speed (racing thoughts,
    verbal overflow) or inhibited speech (blocking, avoidance).
    Both are manifestations of the same underlying state.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

HYPOMANIC / ELEVATED MOOD:

  Acoustic correlates:
  - F0 mean: Raised; often conspicuously bright
  - F0 range: Very wide; dramatic pitch swings
  - Speaking rate: Markedly fast; pressure of speech
  - Loudness: Elevated; difficulty modulating downward
  - Prosody: Over-expressive; exaggerated stress; theatrical quality
  - Articulation: Rapid but often clear (energy driving precision)
  - Pauses: Minimal; speech almost continuous; interrupts own phrases
  - Laughter: Frequent, expansive, potentially inappropriate

  PRESSURE OF SPEECH:
    A clinical term for speech that seems to "push out" at high rate,
    difficult to interrupt. Associated with hypomania and mania.
    Rate can exceed 200 WPM; tangential; topic-jumping mid-sentence.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

IRRITABLE MOOD (chronic irritability, not acute anger):

  Acoustic correlates:
  - F0 mean: Slightly elevated
  - Speech rate: Clipped, slightly faster than usual
  - Articulation: Sharp, hard consonants; dental precision
  - Dynamic range: Compressed but at a higher baseline volume
  - Pauses: Shorter; intolerance of silence
  - Prosody: Blunt, transactional; minimal warmth contours
  - Voice quality: Tight; reduced breathiness; slightly pressed

  DIFFERENCE FROM ANGER:
    Anger spikes. Irritability is a persistent edge — the same
    slightly sharp, abrupt quality maintained across all topics,
    even pleasant ones. It colors everything rather than spiking.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CALM/CONTENT MOOD:

  The baseline many voices aspire to but few maintain consistently.

  Acoustic correlates:
  - F0: Settled at natural optimal pitch; not too high, not too low
  - F0 range: Natural, moderate variation; neither flat nor theatrical
  - Speaking rate: Comfortable; pauses are deliberate and unforced
  - Voice quality: Open resonance; minimal tension; full but not pushed
  - Breathing: Diaphragmatic; regular; unforced
  - Articulation: Clear but relaxed; no over-precision or slurring
  - Prosody: Natural focus stress; sentences feel complete

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FATIGUE / EXHAUSTION:

  Acoustic correlates:
  - F0: Slightly lowered; may drop further at phrase ends
  - F0 range: Narrow; prosody becomes increasingly monotone
  - Voice quality: Increasingly breathy as vocal fold muscle fatigues;
                   "huskiness" develops; roughness may emerge
  - Speaking rate: Slows progressively
  - Articulation: Simplification; consonants weakened; vowels reduced
  - Pauses: Longer; more frequent; spontaneous mid-sentence
  - Resonance: Reduced; voice "retreats" from forward placement
  - Inhalation sounds: More audible; more frequent breath catches
  - Pitch glides: Unintentional pitch drops ("laryngeal sag")

  VOCAL FATIGUE AS A PHENOMENON (separate from general fatigue):
    The laryngeal muscles themselves tire with extended use.
    Symptoms: reduced upper range, increased effort, roughness, pain.
    Professional speakers, teachers, singers face this daily.
    Hydration, technique, and rest are primary countermeasures.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

BOREDOM:

  Often treated as "low arousal" but has distinctive speech character.

  Acoustic correlates:
  - F0: Low, monotone; minimal variation; occasional exaggerated falls
  - Rate: Slowed; drawn out; particular lengthening of vowels
  - Prosody: Listless; minimal stress contrasts
  - Articulation: Relaxed to the point of slurring
  - Voice quality: Breathy; low energy
  - Sighing: Frequent pre-speech sighs or exhalations
  - Elongation: Words stretched without expressive intent

  DISTINGUISHING FROM DEPRESSION:
    Boredom is situationally reversible; voice perks immediately on
    interesting stimulus. Depression persists across contexts.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CONFIDENCE / SELF-ASSURED MOOD:

  Acoustic correlates:
  - F0: Settled at lower end of natural range (authority register)
  - F0 range: Moderate to wide; modulation is deliberate and controlled
  - Rate: Moderately slow; pauses are comfortable, not nervous
  - Intensity: Steady, projecting; does not drop at sentence ends
  - Voice quality: Full, resonant; minimal breathiness; open placement
  - Articulation: Clear, unhurried; complete words; no mumbling
  - Prosody: Declarative intonation even on uncertain content
  - Pauses: Used for emphasis, not hesitation; silence is comfortable

  "POWER VOICE" vs NATURAL CONFIDENCE:
    Performed confidence (power posing voice) often over-corrects:
    excessively lowered pitch, unnaturally slow rate, forced resonance.
    Authentic confidence is marked by the ABSENCE of effortful signaling.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
8.3 MOOD BLENDS AND COMPLEX STATES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Real mood is rarely pure. Blended states produce complex vocal signatures:

MELANCHOLIC NOSTALGIA:
  Low pitch + slow rate (sadness component) + wide melodic F0 range
  (warmth/reminiscing component) + soft volume. The result is a
  voice that is slow and gentle but emotionally rich, not flat.

NERVOUS EXCITEMENT:
  Elevated F0 (anxiety component) + wide range (excitement component)
  + fast rate + irregular rhythm. Unstable but energized quality.
  Common in performers before going on stage.

RESIGNED ACCEPTANCE:
  Flat prosody (no fight left) + moderate or slightly low pitch
  + slow rate + long final falls at phrase ends. Not depressed but
  the voice of someone who has processed loss and arrived at stillness.

SUPPRESSED ANGER / POLITENESS OVER IRRITATION:
  This is perceptually very rich. Two layers fighting:
  - Surface: controlled rate, normal volume, polite prosody
  - Underneath: tighter voice quality, slightly clipped consonants,
    pauses that are just a fraction too controlled, falling intonation
    that ends just slightly too abruptly, micro-increases in F0
  Listeners detect this as "something is off" even when they can't
  name what. The suppression effort itself leaves acoustic traces.

GRIEF (distinct from depression):
  Active grief is not monotone. It involves:
  - Unpredictable pitch breaks and voice cracks (involuntary)
  - Alternation between suppression (tightening) and release (breathiness)
  - Frequent breathing irregularities
  - Sudden intensity changes
  - The voice quality of someone who has been crying: elevated larynx,
    reduced resonance, nasal congestion component, roughness

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
8.4 MOOD AND VOICE: THE FEEDBACK LOOP
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The relationship between mood and voice is bidirectional.

MOOD → VOICE (outgoing):
  Internal state shapes the acoustic output (described above).

VOICE → MOOD (incoming / proprioceptive):
  The act of speaking in a certain way feeds back to reinforce or shift
  internal state. This is the basis of several therapeutic and
  performance techniques:

  - Forcing a smile changes facial muscle feedback and can mildly
    improve mood (facial feedback hypothesis; debated but partially supported)
  - Speaking slowly and deeply induces a calming physiological response
  - Singing activates vagal tone, reducing anxiety
  - Loud, forceful speaking can temporarily increase arousal and confidence
  - Whispering can induce calm in the speaker, not just the listener

  THERAPEUTIC APPLICATIONS:
    Voice work in psychotherapy (e.g., Antoinette Gentile's somatic work)
    deliberately uses prosody and resonance to access and shift mood states.
    EMDR and somatic therapies incorporate vocalisation as a regulatory tool.

VOICE → MOOD IN LISTENERS:
  A speaker's mood-voice complex does not only affect themselves.
  Listeners' physiology, mood, and behavior respond to vocal cues:
  - Low, calm voice reduces listener heart rate
  - Anxious voice raises listener alertness and vigilance
  - Depressed voice can induce emotional contagion (slight mood lowering)
  - Warm, resonant voice triggers oxytocin-mediated relaxation and trust
  This is covered in full in Part X (Social and Interpersonal Effects).

PART IX — PERSONALITY AND ITS STRUCTURAL INFLUENCE ON VOICE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

While mood is a transient state, personality is a stable disposition —
enduring patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving. Personality shapes
not just WHAT a person says but HOW they say it, consistently, across time
and situations. It is the deepest layer of voice after biology.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
9.1 THE BIG FIVE PERSONALITY TRAITS AND VOICE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Big Five (OCEAN) model is the most empirically supported framework.
Each trait has documented vocal correlates.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
EXTRAVERSION (high vs. low)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  HIGH EXTRAVERSION:
  - F0: Higher mean pitch; higher variability
  - Speaking rate: Faster; fewer and shorter pauses
  - Volume: Louder on average; projects without effort
  - Voice quality: More resonant, "forward," energized
  - Turn-taking: Takes longer turns; more likely to interrupt or overlap
  - Articulation: Fluid, expansive; less careful precision
  - Prosody: More expressive; greater use of emphasis and pitch range
  - Laughter: More frequent; more spontaneous; often infectious
  - Silence: Uncomfortable; filled with content or sounds

  LOW EXTRAVERSION (introversion):
  - F0: Moderate to low; less variation
  - Speaking rate: More measured; comfortable with pauses
  - Volume: Softer baseline; modulates downward in groups
  - Voice quality: Often more precise, contained, carefully placed
  - Turn-taking: Waits; shorter turns; more deliberate before speaking
  - Silence: Comfortable; uses pauses as natural pacing
  - Articulation: Often more careful; less reduction

  RESEARCH FINDINGS:
    Extraversion is the personality trait most robustly linked to
    specific vocal features. Studies consistently find that extraverts
    speak louder, faster, more, and with greater pitch variation.
    These features are cross-culturally detected and rated accurately
    by naive listeners after hearing just a few seconds of speech.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
NEUROTICISM (high vs. low / emotional stability)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  HIGH NEUROTICISM:
  - F0: More variable; more susceptible to state-linked fluctuation
  - Voice quality: Tension markers present; slight breathiness or strain
  - Rate: Irregular; can rush or stall under pressure
  - Fillers: More frequent "um," "uh," word retreats
  - Prosody: Emotion leaks readily; difficulty maintaining neutral register
  - Breathing: Shallower; more audible; irregular patterns common
  - Acoustic texture: Less consistent across recordings and contexts

  LOW NEUROTICISM (high stability):
  - F0: Consistent across contexts; not easily disrupted
  - Voice quality: Even, grounded; minimal variation from baseline
  - Prosody: Stable; emotions present but integrated
  - Rate: Consistent; not thrown by challenging questions
  - Breathing: Diaphragmatic; regular

  IMPORTANT NOTE:
    High neuroticism doesn't create one specific voice — it creates
    MORE VARIATION across contexts. The neurotic voice is hard to pin
    down because it keeps shifting. It is most recognizable as
    inconsistency itself.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
OPENNESS TO EXPERIENCE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  HIGH OPENNESS:
  - Lexical richness: More varied, unusual vocabulary
  - Prosody: More creative, less formulaic intonation patterns
  - Speaking style: Digressive; connects unexpected concepts verbally
  - Voice quality: Often slightly more "aesthetic" care in expression
  - Rate: Moderate to varied; pauses for reflection are common
  - Metaphor and abstraction: High; talks in concepts
  - Hedging: More qualifications, nuance, "or rather," "in a sense"

  LOW OPENNESS:
  - Lexical: More conventional vocabulary; fewer unusual word choices
  - Prosody: More predictable, less creative intonation
  - Speech style: Direct, concrete, practical
  - Rate: Consistent and purposeful

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSCIENTIOUSNESS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  HIGH CONSCIENTIOUSNESS:
  - Articulation: Clear, deliberate; careful pronunciation
  - Rate: Measured; rarely rushed; finishes sentences
  - Prosody: Organized; signposts structure ("first... second... finally...")
  - Pauses: Used deliberately before important points
  - Self-correction: Corrects slips; restates when imprecise
  - Volume: Appropriate to context; well-calibrated

  LOW CONSCIENTIOUSNESS:
  - Articulation: More casual, reduced
  - Rate: More variable; can rush or trail off
  - Prosody: Less structured; tangential; unfinished sentences common
  - Self-correction: Fewer corrections; comfortable with imprecision

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
AGREEABLENESS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  HIGH AGREEABLENESS:
  - F0: Slightly higher; more melodic, "warm" prosody
  - Voice quality: Softer; less assertive; more inviting
  - Volume: Moderate; rarely dominates acoustically
  - Rate: Accommodates interlocutor; slows when others slow
  - Turn-taking: Yields readily; uses back-channel signals ("mm-hmm," "yeah")
  - Fillers: Agreement sounds frequent and warm
  - Hedging: More frequent ("maybe," "I think," "could be")
  - Laughter: Supportive, others-affirming

  LOW AGREEABLENESS:
  - F0: Often lower; more assertive tonal patterns
  - Volume: Louder, more dominant
  - Rate: Less accommodating to others' tempo
  - Turn-taking: Holds floor; fewer back-channels
  - Hedging: Minimal; direct, unqualified statements
  - Tone: Can read as cold, blunt, aggressive

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
9.2 DARK TRIAD PERSONALITY TRAITS AND VOICE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Dark Triad (Narcissism, Machiavellianism, Psychopathy) has specific
vocal research and strong lay intuitions about voice quality.

NARCISSISM:
  - Voice quality: Often trained or "polished"-seeming; self-aware of voice
  - F0: Context-dependent; highly controlled in performance, more erratic
    in unguarded moments
  - Volume: Loud; tends to dominate acoustic space
  - Rate: Confident, unhurried (when performing); rapid when grandiose
  - Prosody: Dramatic; uses emphasis performatively
  - Laughter: Often sharp, brief, or at others' expense
  - Listening behaviors: Back-channels minimal; tends to re-route to self
  - Content: Frequent self-reference; voice that "centers" itself

PSYCHOPATHY:
  - Voice quality: Notably flat affect in primary psychopathy;
                   the voice is acoustically correct but emotionally empty
  - F0 range: Reduced; emotional prosody is shallow or mimicked
  - Mimicry: High — can produce apparently warm, authentic emotional prosody
             that nonetheless fails to modulate with appropriate timing
  - Rate: Confident; rarely stumbles; smooth
  - Pauses: Strategic; silence is comfortable and used as control
  - The "uncanny" voice: Listeners often report something feeling "off"
    before consciously identifying what — the timing of emotional cues
    is slightly delayed, slightly learned rather than spontaneous

MACHIAVELLIANISM:
  - Voice: Deliberate; controlled; adapts to audience
  - Chameleon quality: Shifts register, vocabulary, warmth to match target
  - Tells: Under stress, slight over-control appears — too measured,
    too consistent, implausible calm

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
9.3 ATTACHMENT STYLE AND VOICE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Attachment theory (Bowlby/Ainsworth) describes how early relational experiences
create persistent patterns that affect how people speak in close or
emotionally significant contexts.

SECURE ATTACHMENT:
  - Voice: Open, direct, moderately expressive
  - Emotional topics: Voice stays regulated; neither over- nor under-expressive
  - Vulnerability: Can be expressed without vocal collapse or rigid control
  - Conflict: Voice rises appropriately but recovers quickly

ANXIOUS/PREOCCUPIED ATTACHMENT:
  - Voice: More variable; emotionally reactive; prone to rising pitch
  - Emotional topics: Voice escalates quickly; may crack or rush
  - Self-interruptions: More frequent; emotional flooding disrupts fluency
  - Seeking reassurance: Prosodic appeals ("...right?" "...don't you think?")

AVOIDANT/DISMISSING ATTACHMENT:
  - Voice: Flatter; emotional topics produce minimal vocal change
  - F0 range: Compressed on emotional content
  - Rate: Often speeds up slightly when emotional topics arise
    (moving through quickly, not dwelling)
  - Intellectual prosody: Topics reframed abstractly; voice stays analytical

DISORGANIZED ATTACHMENT:
  - Voice: Least predictable; sudden shifts; fragmented prosody
  - May show brief collapses in voice quality or intonation
  - Pattern: Oscillation between activation and shutdown vocal modes

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
9.4 PERSONALITY AS VOCAL LAYER
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

For the purpose of voice reproduction, personality operates as a stable
filter applied below situational mood and above biological baseline.

The reproduction model must account for:

  BIOLOGICAL BASELINE (anatomy, age, sex, health)
         ↓
  PERSONALITY LAYER (stable dispositions: rate preference, range preference,
                     resonance habits, articulation style, turn-taking)
         ↓
  MOOD LAYER (diffuse sustained background tone)
         ↓
  EMOTIONAL LAYER (acute, event-triggered changes)
         ↓
  SITUATIONAL/CONTEXTUAL LAYER (audience, setting, purpose)
         ↓
  WHAT WE ACTUALLY HEAR

  Missing the personality layer = voice that is acoustically accurate
  in isolation but feels "inconsistently in character" across contexts.

PART X — ENVIRONMENTAL CONTEXT AND ITS EFFECT ON VOICE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The environment in which speech occurs is not a neutral backdrop.
It fundamentally shapes every dimension of vocal behavior — physically,
socially, and psychologically.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
10.1 ACOUSTIC ENVIRONMENT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE LOMBARD EFFECT:
  One of the most robust findings in vocal behavior research.
  When background noise increases, speakers automatically increase their
  vocal intensity (volume), often without conscious awareness.

  Properties:
  - Automatic; not fully suppressible even with deliberate effort
  - Accompanied by: F0 rise, increased spectral tilt, clearer articulation
  - Magnitude: roughly +3 to +6 dB for every +10 dB of background noise
  - Also affects rate (slower), vowel duration (longer), and consonant clarity

  PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS FOR REPRODUCTION:
    A voice recorded in a quiet studio sounds fundamentally different
    from the same voice on a street. To reproduce a voice authentically
    in context, the Lombard adjustment must be modeled.

ROOM ACOUSTICS:
  The physical space a voice occupies changes its character through:

  Reverberation:
    Sound reflections from walls, floor, ceiling.
    RT60 (reverberation time): How long it takes for sound to decay 60 dB.
    - Dead room (anechoic): No reflections; voice sounds small, close, dry
    - Normal room (~0.3s RT60): Natural; warm
    - Large hall (~1.5s RT60): Voice sounds large, distant, majestic
    - Cathedral (~5–8s RT60): Voice smears; words blend; reverberant aura

    Speakers unconsciously adapt to reverberant spaces:
    - Slow down (to avoid word smearing)
    - Increase pauses
    - Articulate more clearly
    - Lower volume (excessive loudness in reverberant space is unpleasant)

  Echo:
    Discrete reflections with delay >50ms. Perceived as a repeat.
    Speakers exposed to prominent echo reduce rate, may lose fluency.

  Absorption:
    Soft furnishings, carpets, curtains absorb sound.
    A voice in a furnished room sounds warmer, more intimate.
    A voice in a bare concrete room sounds harsher, more aggressive.

  Directionality / Proximity:
    The proximity effect (microphone physics): Low frequencies increase
    disproportionately as the speaker moves closer to a microphone.
    This creates intimacy cues even when unintentional.
    Radio presenters and ASMR creators exploit this deliberately.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
10.2 SOCIAL ENVIRONMENT: WHO IS PRESENT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE OBSERVER EFFECT IN SPEECH:
  The presence of an audience — even one silent person — changes voice.
  This is not paranoia; it is a normal social regulation mechanism.

AUDIENCE SIZE:
  - Solo (self-talk or recording alone): Most natural; least monitored
  - One-on-one: Intimate register; maximum accommodation to specific person
  - Small group (3–7): Register negotiation; voice must carry while staying personal
  - Medium audience (10–50): Projection increases; formality rises; prosody broadens
  - Large audience (100+): Formal register; slower rate; amplified gestures in voice
  - Mass audience (broadcast): Paradoxically sometimes MORE intimate (speaking
    to "one person" in millions of homes; anchors are trained this way)

AUDIENCE IDENTITY:

  Stranger vs. familiar person:
    Voice with strangers: More careful pronunciation; fewer slang terms;
    more formal prosody; less breathiness; more controlled rate.
    Voice with intimates: Reduced forms; more breathiness; private vocabulary;
    higher tolerance for silence; more emotional leakage.

  Power differential:
    Speaking to someone of higher status/power:
    - F0 often rises slightly (subconscious deference signal)
    - Rate may slow (more deliberate, careful)
    - Formality increases
    - Volume often decreases slightly
    - More hedging language acoustically expressed through
      non-final intonation patterns

    Speaking to someone of lower status:
    - F0 may drop (authority signal)
    - Rate may slow deliberately (clarity instruction mode)
    - Volume may increase
    - More declarative intonation
    - Less self-interruption

  Gender dynamics:
    Cross-gender vocal accommodation is well documented.
    In mixed-gender groups, many women raise F0 slightly; many men
    lower F0 slightly (both moving toward perceived gender norms).
    This is not universal; it is socially and culturally contingent.

  Emotional state of the listener:
    Speakers calibrate to the emotional cues they receive.
    Speaking to someone visibly distressed: voice softens, slows, drops in pitch.
    Speaking to someone excited: voice accelerates, pitch rises, range widens.
    Speaking to someone hostile: voice may tighten, rate changes (individual variation).

ACCOMMODATION THEORY (Giles):
  People continuously adjust their speech toward or away from their
  interlocutor's style (convergence vs. divergence).

  CONVERGENCE: Moving speech toward the other person's style.
    Functions: Create rapport, signal belonging, increase liking.
    Features: Matching rate, pitch level, vocabulary, accent features.

  DIVERGENCE: Moving speech away from the other person's style.
    Functions: Mark identity, assert distinction, create distance.
    Example: Speaking MORE in home dialect when confronted by an outsider.

  HYPERCONVERGENCE:
    Over-matching, especially downward (speaking "more simply" than necessary).
    Can be perceived as condescending. Common with elderly, children, disabled.
    "Baby talk" (infant-directed speech / motherese) is an extreme form.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
10.3 INFANT-DIRECTED SPEECH (Motherese / Child-Directed Speech)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A highly distinctive speech register universally found across cultures.
Adults (not only mothers) shift to this register when addressing infants.

ACOUSTIC CHARACTERISTICS:
  - F0 mean: Significantly elevated (often an octave above normal adult speech)
  - F0 range: Dramatically widened (sing-song, exaggerated contours)
  - Rate: Slower; shorter utterances; more repetition
  - Pauses: Longer, more frequent; invites "turns" even before language
  - Articulation: Hyper-articulated; vowels expanded ("hyperarticulation")
  - Volume: Often softer; or rhythmically varied
  - Vowels: More clearly differentiated; more peripheral in vowel space
  - Repetition: Phrases and prosodic shapes repeated; melodic looping

FUNCTIONS:
  - Attracts and maintains infant attention (high F0 is salient)
  - Communicates emotional warmth and safety
  - Scaffolds language acquisition (exaggerated vowels help infants map phonology)
  - Regulates infant arousal (calm version) or stimulation (playful version)

ADULT-TO-ADULT VERSION:
  Modified forms of infant-directed prosody appear in:
  - Romantic partner speech ("baby talk" between intimates)
  - Speech to pets (very similar acoustic profile)
  - Care contexts (nursing homes, certain therapeutic contexts)
  These are social bonding signals, not infantilization per se.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
10.4 PHYSICAL ENVIRONMENT: SPACE, TEMPERATURE, HEALTH CONDITIONS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

TEMPERATURE AND VOICE:
  Cold:
  - Laryngeal and articulatory muscles stiffen in cold air
  - Rate slows; articulatory precision drops
  - Voice quality may become slightly tighter
  - Cold dry air desiccates mucous membranes → reduced vocal fold lubrication
    → increased friction → roughness, vocal fatigue

  Heat:
  - Dehydration risk; dry vocal folds → same effects as cold air
  - General fatigue at extreme heat → speech rate drops, energy reduces
  - Humid warmth: If well-hydrated, can improve vocal resonance

ALTITUDE:
  - Reduced atmospheric pressure means less air density
  - Breathing requires more effort
  - High altitude voice: more frequent breathing pauses; slightly quieter;
    breathiness may increase; greater respiratory effort audible

HYDRATION:
  Critically underappreciated. The vocal folds require hydration from two sources:
  - Systemic hydration (drinking water): takes time to reach fold surface
  - Surface (nebulized) hydration: more immediate effect
  
  Dehydrated voice:
  - Increased jitter and shimmer
  - Reduced HNR (more noise)
  - Higher minimum phonation threshold (requires more effort to vibrate)
  - Rougher quality; reduced upper range
  - Faster vocal fatigue

ILLNESS (Cold, Allergy, Infection):
  Upper respiratory illness profoundly alters voice:
  - Laryngitis: Vocal fold edema/swelling → lower F0, roughness, breathiness,
                reduced range; in severe cases, aphonia (no voice)
  - Nasal congestion: Hyponasality; "stuffed up" quality; /m/, /n/, /ŋ/ affected
  - Post-nasal drip: Throat-clearing tics; irritation-driven voice breaks
  - Coughing: Mechanical trauma to folds; increases roughness
  - Fatigue from illness: All fatigue vocal signs amplified

POSTURE AND BODY POSITION:
  Posture directly affects phonation and resonance.

  Upright (standing or good seated posture):
  - Diaphragm has full range of motion
  - Trachea and larynx in optimal alignment
  - Maximum resonance available

  Slouched/forward head posture:
  - Compresses chest; restricts diaphragmatic movement
  - Pulls larynx forward and down; changes resonance geometry
  - Associated with reduced vocal projection, breathier quality

  Lying down:
  - Gravity assists or opposes breathing differently
  - Voice sounds different when supine vs. prone vs. upright

  PRACTICAL:
    The common advice "sit up straight when speaking" has genuine
    acoustic justification, not just social convention.

PAIN:
  Physical pain (chronic or acute) affects voice in predictable ways:
  - Tension increases throughout the body, including laryngeal muscles
  - F0 rises slightly (tension effect)
  - Prosody becomes more compressed; emotional expression suppressed
  - Rate slows if pain is severe (concentration and energy diverted)
  - Breathing becomes shallower (guarding against pain-triggering deep breaths)

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
10.5 CULTURAL AND SITUATIONAL ENVIRONMENT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CULTURAL NORMS FOR VOICE:

  Volume norms:
    Different cultures have different ambient volume expectations.
    Northern European norms (Finnish, Swedish, Norwegian): Quiet; silence valued
    Mediterranean norms (Italian, Greek, Southern Spanish): Louder; overlapping normal
    American norms: Moderate-loud; silence often filled
    Japanese norms: Soft; context-dependent; "outside voice" vs "inside voice" strict

  Silence norms:
    Cultures vary in their tolerance for conversational silence (pause).
    Finnish culture: Long pauses (up to several seconds) are comfortable; normal.
    American culture: Gaps over ~1 second are uncomfortable; rushed to fill.
    Japanese culture: Silence can be a complete communicative act.

    In cross-cultural interaction, silence tolerance mismatches cause constant
    vocal behavior adjustment — and often mutual irritation.

  Emotional display rules:
    Some cultures expect emotional prosody to be suppressed (emotional
    stoicism norms: British "stiff upper lip," East Asian contexts).
    Others expect and reward full emotional vocal display.
    The same internal state produces different vocal outputs depending on
    culturally learned display rules.

  Pitch norms:
    Some cultures consider a high F0 in men to signal weakness or youth;
    others find it warm and approachable.
    Female F0 norms vary: Japan has significantly higher habitual F0 in
    women than Western cultures, partly driven by social expectation.

FORMALITY OF SITUATION:
  A single speaker can sound like a different person across:
  - Job interview vs. drinks with old friends
  - Eulogy vs. football match
  - Classroom presentation vs. bedroom conversation
  - Phone call to government office vs. texting voice messages

  Formality shifts:
  - Pronunciation: More careful; fewer reduced forms
  - Rate: Slower; more deliberate
  - Volume: More controlled; appropriate projection
  - Vocabulary: Shift to more formal register
  - Prosody: More structured; more signposting intonation
  - Fillers: Fewer "um"s; replaced by more formal hesitations or rephrasing

PHONE/VIDEO CALL VS. IN-PERSON:
  Telephone removes visual information, forcing speakers to compensate:
  - Over-articulate more
  - Speak slightly louder
  - Use more verbal acknowledgment sounds (since head nods are invisible)
  - More explicit prosodic signaling (since facial expression unavailable)

  Video call (fatigue):
    Extended video calls create a specific fatigue from mirror self-focus,
    reduced peripheral stimulation, and constrained movement.
    Voice in later video calls: flatter prosody, slower rate, reduced volume,
    more frequent pauses. "Zoom fatigue" has a distinct vocal signature.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
10.6 HOW ENVIRONMENT INTERACTS WITH THE OTHER LAYERS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Environment does not operate in isolation. It modulates every other layer:

  PERSONALITY × ENVIRONMENT:
    An extravert's voice changes less across contexts than an introvert's.
    A highly neurotic person shows more environmental sensitivity in their voice.
    A conscientious person maintains more consistent articulation across contexts.

  MOOD × ENVIRONMENT:
    A mildly depressed person in a loud, stimulating social environment may
    temporarily shift toward a more activated vocal profile. Environment can
    override or modulate mood — temporarily. The underlying mood resurfaces
    when stimulation drops.

  EMOTION × ENVIRONMENT:
    Anger expressed in a library is suppressed and changes character.
    Anger expressed on a football pitch is amplified and released.
    The same emotion is acoustically different depending on environmental
    permission and constraint.

  ACCENT × ENVIRONMENT:
    Speakers in their home region show stronger accent features.
    Speakers outside their home region often moderate their accent
    (upward accommodation toward prestige norm).
    Under stress, emotional pressure, or with same-region friends,
    home accent features resurface regardless of usual moderation.

PART XI — SOCIAL AND INTERPERSONAL EFFECTS: HOW VOICE AFFECTS OTHERS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
11.1 VOCAL ATTRACTIVENESS AND SOCIAL JUDGMENT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Voice is a powerful social signal. Listeners form rapid, persistent
impressions from brief vocal samples — often within 100–500 milliseconds.

ATTRACTIVENESS:
  Acoustic features associated with perceived vocal attractiveness:
  - Males: Lower F0 (within natural range); moderate breathiness; smooth quality
  - Females: Higher F0; moderate breathiness; slight resonance warmth
  - Both: HNR (clean voice); moderate speech rate; natural prosodic variation
  
  These preferences have partial evolutionary explanations (F0 correlates
  with body size and hormonal profiles) but are also heavily culturally
  mediated and individually variable.

COMPETENCE:
  Perceived competence correlates with:
  - Lower F0 (authority signal)
  - Consistent rate and volume (self-regulation signal)
  - Clear articulation
  - Appropriate pausing (thinking, not stumbling)
  - Declarative intonation (certainty signal)
  
  Upspeak (high rising terminal) reduces perceived competence in most
  Western professional contexts, regardless of actual content.

TRUSTWORTHINESS:
  Correlates with:
  - Moderate, stable F0 (not too high — deception signal; not too low — threat)
  - Good HNR (breathiness in excess can read as concealment)
  - Natural prosodic variation (flat affect triggers suspicion)
  - Appropriate response latency (too fast = rehearsed; too slow = hiding)

DOMINANCE:
  Correlates with:
  - Lower F0
  - Louder volume
  - Slower rate
  - Longer turn duration
  - Comfortable silence (not filling pauses)
  - Other-directed prosody that does not seek confirmation

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
11.2 EMOTIONAL CONTAGION THROUGH VOICE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Voice directly induces emotional states in listeners through multiple pathways:

MIRROR NEURON SYSTEM:
  Hearing voice activates motor and emotional processing circuits in the
  listener that partially simulate the speaker's state.
  A trembling, fearful voice activates mild vigilance in the listener.
  A warm, calm voice activates parasympathetic relaxation.

PHYSIOLOGICAL SYNCHRONY:
  In extended interactions, listener breathing, heart rate, and F0 patterns
  gradually synchronize with the speaker's. This is "physiological coupling"
  and underlies the deep sense of "connection" in good conversations.

AUTONOMIC RESPONSE TO VOICE TYPES:
  High-pitched screaming: Immediate sympathetic activation (universal; processed
                          by amygdala before cortical awareness)
  Infant cry: Specific rapid autonomic and cortical response in adults;
              female caregivers show larger brain activation
  Low, slow, regular voice: Parasympathetic activation; heart rate drops
  Harsh, erratic voice: Sustained sympathetic arousal; vigilance maintained

THERAPEUTIC VOICE EFFECTS:
  - Music therapists exploit F0, rhythm, and vocal quality to regulate patients
  - Lullabies cross-culturally share acoustic features: slow, descending, regular
  - "Calm voice" interventions in schools, hospitals, crisis settings are
    evidence-based applications of these principles

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
11.3 THE VOICE IN PERSUASION AND INFLUENCE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Political and persuasive research consistently shows that HOW something
is said matters as much as, or more than, WHAT is said.

KEY FINDINGS:
  - Speeches delivered with more pitch variation are rated more persuasive
  - Moderate speaking rate is perceived as more credible than very fast
    or very slow speech
  - Voices that end phrases on falling intonation are rated more confident
    and believable than rising intonation
  - Smiling while speaking is detectable acoustically and increases likeability
  - Volume that slightly exceeds conversational baseline signals conviction
  - Pausing before a key point gives it weight (the "pregnant pause")

THE PROSODY OF LEADERSHIP:
  - Effective leaders tend to use downward intonation on key points
  - They use silence strategically rather than filling it
  - They maintain consistent voice quality under pressure
  - They modulate warmth vs. authority prosody fluidly

THE VOICE IN DECEPTION:
  Despite popular belief, no single acoustic cue reliably identifies lying.
  However, under the cognitive load of constructing a false narrative:
  - Rate often decreases slightly
  - Pitch may rise slightly
  - Pauses become longer and more frequent
  - Verbal fluency decreases (more fillers, more restarts)
  - These effects are small, inconsistent, and easily trained away

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
11.4 VOICE AND RELATIONSHIP DYNAMICS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE VOICE OF INTIMACY:
  Close relationships develop private vocal registers:
  - Shared pitch ranges and rhythms
  - Private vocabulary with specific prosodic signatures
  - Mutual back-channel signals unique to the pair
  - Tolerance for unusual silence patterns
  
  These "vocal dialects of intimacy" are highly recognizable markers of
  relationship depth. Strangers performing them artificially sound uncanny.

VOCAL SYNCHRONY AS RELATIONSHIP INDICATOR:
  Research shows that F0 synchrony during conversation correlates with:
  - Relationship quality
  - Rapport and trust
  - Therapeutic alliance (in therapy pairs)
  - Team cohesion (in group settings)

  Higher synchrony = deeper connection in most contexts.

VOCAL ASYMMETRY AS POWER INDICATOR:
  When one speaker consistently accommodates (converges) and the other does
  not, the asymmetry maps onto social power.
  The higher-status person's voice is accommodated to.
  The lower-status person accommodates.
  This plays out in every conversation, largely unconsciously.

APPENDIX E — UPDATED COMPLETE VOICE REPRODUCTION BLUEPRINT (v2)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The original 6-phase blueprint (Part VII) is now extended with the
mood, personality, and environmental layers:

  PHASE 0: DATA COLLECTION
  □ Gather recordings across MULTIPLE CONTEXTS (not just one register)
  □ Include: quiet 1-on-1, public/group, emotional, fatigued if possible
  □ Note the environmental conditions of each recording

  PHASE 1: ACOUSTIC PROFILING (unchanged — see Part VII)

  PHASE 2: PHONOLOGICAL PROFILING (unchanged — see Part VII)

  PHASE 3: PROSODIC PROFILING (unchanged — see Part VII)

  PHASE 4: VOICE QUALITY PROFILING (unchanged — see Part VII)

  PHASE 5: BEHAVIORAL PROFILING (unchanged — see Part VII)

  PHASE 6 [NEW]: MOOD PROFILING
  □ What is this person's typical baseline mood state?
  □ Does their voice show depressive, anxious, elevated, or neutral baseline?
  □ How wide is their mood-driven vocal range?
  □ Can contextual mood shifts be detected and mapped?
  □ Are there recurring emotional colorings across recordings?

  PHASE 7 [NEW]: PERSONALITY LAYER PROFILING
  □ Estimate Big Five profile from vocal behavior (see Part IX)
  □ Note extraversion-related volume, rate, and range habits
  □ Note neuroticism-related consistency or variability
  □ Note agreeableness in turn-taking and accommodation patterns
  □ Note conscientiousness in articulation and self-correction behavior
  □ Identify any Dark Triad signals if relevant

  PHASE 8 [NEW]: ENVIRONMENTAL SENSITIVITY PROFILING
  □ Does the voice change dramatically across environments or stay stable?
  □ What is the Lombard effect magnitude for this speaker?
  □ How does the voice change with audience size and status?
  □ Does the speaker accommodate to interlocutors rapidly or resist?
  □ Note any cultural background affecting volume, silence, and display norms

  PHASE 9: SYNTHESIS/REPRODUCTION
  □ For AI: Multi-style model conditioned on context embeddings
  □ For human: Practice across contextual range, not just neutral register
  □ Test reproduction in varied contexts: intimate, stressed, formal, fatigued
  □ Verify that the PERSONALITY LAYER is stable across contexts in the reproduction
  □ Verify that MOOD/EMOTION shifts behave as expected given the profile
  □ Blind contextual test: Does the reproduction "fit" in contexts
    it was not directly trained/practiced on?
